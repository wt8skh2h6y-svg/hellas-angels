01 · Wednesday's Story

A girl named Wednesday.

Wednesday Hellebore — Hella, to those who knew her — was with me through major moves, relationships, and some of the hardest stretches of my life. Fierce, bossy, fearless, and deeply loved. I was her person, and she loved me back: in biscuits, in strange hooting noises from the next room until my "I'm in here!" sent her sprinting onto my lap, in yowls of pride when she fished the hair trap out of the bathtub (it was always somewhere different).

She was a weirdo — rescued from a dumpster as a tiny kitten by my eclectic neighbor and carried to my door in a fanny-pack, then raised by me. How could she not be a little strange?

She ran the household. Half the size of some of the other cats, she kept everyone in line with a swift, soft-pawed wallop upside the head — and they respected her for it. If someone came to the door she would growl (growl!) and rush the window to see who dared trespass. She was never one to sit in the middle of things asking for attention; she watched from the periphery, perched at the top of the stairs, yellow lantern-eyes surveying all, judging all.

In November, a coyote killed her in the alley behind my house. I heard her cries and ran, but I wasn't fast enough. I can only hope it was quick.

The next day I buried her, and made a promise: her life, and her loss, would mean something bigger.

A hellebore is a winter flower — it blooms when nothing else will, in the cold months when everything else has given up. People used to plant them by doorways for protection, and tuck them into burial grounds as a quiet promise that something tender could outlast the dark.

That is who she was.

02 · Where things stand

This is also about survival.

The cats

Eleven indoor cats live in my home right now. A handful of semi-feral neighborhood cats rotate through my front porch, fed and watched daily. My immediate goal is to stabilize the home colony with vaccinations, bordetella testing and treatment, and basic welfare. After that: TNR and broader rescue.

The accident

In March, I was injured in a hit-and-run that totaled my car. Since then I've been balancing ongoing neck, back, and nerve pain, medical appointments, transportation problems, and the physical demands of continuing to work while still caring for these animals. Frankly: it's a lot.

The day job, and an unlikely advantage

I'm a USPS mail carrier — out in Dayton's neighborhoods every day, on foot, paying attention. Mail carriers, delivery workers, and residents see what happens in a neighborhood long before any formal system does. Long-term, I want to use that everyday presence to spot at-risk cats, colonies, loose dogs, and injured or lost animals — and route that information to the local groups already equipped to help. Right now, the priority is just keeping the household afloat.

The longer road

Something that lasts.

The hope is bigger than one block. A grassroots rescue and welfare project that uses my daily route through Dayton — and the eyes of other carriers, delivery workers, and neighbors — to identify at-risk cats, colonies, and the corners of the city where stepping in early could save a life. A reporting tool. A volunteer network. Eventually, real 501(c)(3) status. None of that is here yet — but it's the road, and every donation moves us further along it.

For now: feed, trap, treat, love.
